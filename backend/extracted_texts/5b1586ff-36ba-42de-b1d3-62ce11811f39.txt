Isomers
•Isomers are different compounds with the same molecular formula.
•There are several types of isomers in organic chemistry.
•The two large classes of isomers are:
i.Constitutionalisomers
ii.Stereoisomers

Constitutional Isomers
•Constitutional isomers (or structural isomers) are isomers that differ 
in their bonding sequence, that is, their atoms are connected 
differently, but molecular formula is the same.
•For example, the branching of an alkane.
•Isomers have different physical and chemical properties.

Stereoisomers
•Stereoisomers are isomers that differ only in how their atoms are 
arranged in space.
•I.e. same connection of atoms and bonds, but different spatial 
arrangement of the atoms.
•An important class of stereoisomers are the geometric isomers.
•For example cis-and trans-2-butene are geometric isomers, because 
they have the same connectivity of bonds, but different spatial 
orientation of the groups attached to the double bond.

But-1-ene and but-2-ene are constitutional isomers.
Cis-but-2-ene and trans-but-2-ene are stereoisomers 
(geometric isomers).

•Due to rigidity of their bonds, cycloalkanes also exhibit cis-and trans-
isomerism.
•A cycloalkane has two distinct faces, the upper face and the lower 
face.
•If two same substituents point towards the same face, it is known as a 
cisisomer.
•If two same substituents point towards opposite faces, it is known as 
a transisomer.

•Another group of stereoisomers are the chiral isomers.
•Objects that are non-superimposable mirror images of each other are 
said to be chiral.
•For example, your left hand and your right hand are non-
superimposable mirror images of each other –therefore they are 
chiral.
•Objects that have mirror images that are superimposable are said to 
be achiral.

Chiral objects

•Like other objects, molecules are either chiral or achiral.
•Two molecules are said to be superimposableif they can be placed 
on top of each other and the three dimensional position of each atom 
of one molecule coincides with the equivalent atom of the other 
molecule. 
•To draw the mirror image of a molecule, simply draw the same 
structure with left and right side of the molecule reversed.
•The mirror image isomers of a chiral molecule are known as 
enantiomers.

•How do you know if a molecule is chiral or not?
•The most common feature that leads to chirality is a carbon atom 
that is bonded to four different groups.
•Such a carbon atom is known as an asymmetric carbon atom, or a 
chiral carbon atom, and is designated with an asterix(*)
