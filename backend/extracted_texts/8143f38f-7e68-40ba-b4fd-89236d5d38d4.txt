Classification of Markets
ÔĀĪOn the Basis of Location or Place of Operation
a) Village Market:Located in a small village, where buyers and sellers
normally reside in that village
b) Urban Markets:Located in towns near the centres of production of
agricultural commodities.
c) Central Markets:located generally at district headquarters or
important trading centres
d) Terminal Markets:
ÔÉľLocated where the products are either finally disposed off to consumers or
processors, or assembled for export.
ÔÉľLocated either in metropolitan cities or at seaports.

Classification of Markets...
ÔĀĪOn the Basis of Area/Coverage:
a) Local:A market confined within a village or group of villages.
b) Regional Markets:A market which covers a larger area (e.g. food grain market
in central region of Malawi)
c) National Markets:A market which covers an entire nation (e.g. Tobacco
Marketing)
d) World or International Market:
ÔāßA market in which buyers and sellers are drawn from more than one country
ÔāßExist for commodities which have a world-wide demand and/or supply (e.g. fuel, coffee, tea,
sugar, machinery).

Classification of Markets...
ÔĀĪOn the Basis of Time Span:
a) Short period Markets:
ÔāßHeld only for a day or few hours
ÔāßThe products dealt with are of a highly perishable nature, such as fish, fresh
vegetables, and liquid milk.
b) Periodic Markets:
ÔāßCongregation of buyers and sellers takes place at specified places either in
villages, semi-urban areas or some parts of urban areas on specific days and time.
c) Long-period Markets:
ÔāßHeld for a longer period than the short-period markets.
ÔāßCommodities traded are less perishable and can be stored for some time; like
food grains and oilseeds.

Classification of Markets...
ÔĀĪOn the Basis of Volumes of Transactions
ÔāßWholesale Markets:
ÔÉľOne in which commodities are bought and sold in large lots or in bulk.
ÔāßRetail Markets:
ÔÉľA retail market is one in which commodities are bought from wholesalers and
sold in small lots to consumers
ÔÉľDistinction between wholesale and retail markets can be made mainly on the
basis of buyer.
ÔÉľIn a retail market buyers are generally ultimate consumers, whereas in the
wholesale market buyers can be wholesalers or retailers. But sometimes-bulk
consumers also purchase from the wholesale markets.
ÔÉľQuantity transacted in retail markets is generally smaller than in wholesale
markets.

b) Specialized Markets:

Classification of Markets...
ÔĀĪOn the Basis of Nature of Transactions
ÔāßSpot or Cash Markets:A market in which goods are exchanged for money
immediately after a sale.
ÔāßForward Markets:A market in which purchase and sale agreement takes place
now but the actual exchange of the commodity takes place on some specified
date in future.

Classification of Markets...
ÔĀĪOn the Basis of Extent of Public Intervention
a) Regulated Markets:
ÔāßThese markets operate in accordance with specific rules and regulations framed
by Government institutions/agencies-
Ôāße.g. Tobacco Commission (TC) controls all tobacco marketing in Malawi
b) Unregulated Markets:
ÔāßThese are markets in which business is conducted without any set rules and
regulations(e.g. fish marketing in Malawi; illegal artisanal mining).
ÔāßTraders themselves frame the rules for conducting business and running the
markets.
ÔāßImperfections are common in these markets, ranging from unstandardized charges
for marketing functions to imperfect determination of prices.

ÔĀĪDegree of Product Differentiation
ÔÉľNature of products affect market structure - homogeneous or
heterogeneous.
ÔÉľHomogeneous products do not significantly affect price variations of

Market Structures...
ÔÉľBased on the 5 market determinants, markets are placed on a continuous
scale: from perfectly competitive (pure competition) to pure monopoly
(Imperfect competition).
ÔĀĪPerfect/Pure Competition Market:
ÔÉľIt‚Äôs a market where there are a large number of buyers and sellers;
ÔÉľAll buyers and sellers have perfect information (about demand, supply and
prices);
ÔÉľThere is free entry & exit of firms in the market
ÔÉľProducts are homogeneous.
ÔĀĪImperfect Markets
ÔÉľMarkets in which the conditions of perfect competition are lacking are

Market Structures...
Sub-Categories of Imperfect Markets:
ÔĀĪMonopoly Market:
ÔāßA market with only one seller of a product.
ÔāßIn this market the seller has absolute control over quantity and price of the product.
ÔāßPrice of the product is generally higher than in competitive markets
ÔāßA market with one buyer is known as amonopsonymarket(e.g. the formal milk
market in Malawi)
ÔĀĪDuopoly Market:
ÔāßA market with only two sellers of a product/commodity(e.g. the Sugar Market in
Malawi).
ÔāßIt is easy for the two sellers to collude to charge a common price that would be
higher than in an ideal competitive market.

Market structures...Sub-categories of Imperfect Markets
ÔĀĪOligopolistic Market:
ÔÉľIt is a market that has more than two but still a few sellers of a product(e.g.
telecommunication market; banking sector).
ÔÉľA market having a few (more than two) buyers is known asoligopsony
market(e.g. tobacco market; cotton market; tea industry).
ÔĀĪMonopolistic Competition Market:
ÔÉľIt is a market that has many sellers, with each seller having heterogeneous
and differentiated products.
ÔÉľThe difference is made conspicuous by different features on the product.
ÔÉľDifferent prices prevail for the same basic product.
ÔāßExamples:agricultural markets, car industry, fashion/clothing industry.
