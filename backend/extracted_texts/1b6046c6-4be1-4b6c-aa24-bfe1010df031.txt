Mechanism of alkene halogenation
Electrophilic addition of the halogen: the double bond attacks the 
electrophilic nucleus of one halogen atom forming a new covalent bond 
with that halogen atom, while the other halogen atom leaves with both 
electrons from the X—X bond. At the same time, the halogen atom uses 
its lone pair of electrons to form a bond with the second carbon atom of 
the double bond. 
This results in the formation of a haloniumion.

A haloniumion is a three membered ring with a positive charge on the 
halogen atom.
Reactions with bromine form bromoniumions, with chlorine form 
chloroniumions, etc.

The haloniumion is extremely unstable and highly electrophilic.

electrophilic carbon of the haloniumion, causing the ring to break 
open to form a stable halogenated product.

Hydrogenation of Alkenes
•Hydrogen adds across a double bond to give an alkane.
•This is a reduction reaction.
•The process usually requires a catalyst containing Pd, Pt, or Ni.

•The reaction is carried out by dissolving the alkene in ethanol or 
another non-reacting organic solvent, adding the solid catalyst, and 
exposing the mixture to hydrogen gas at pressures of about 1 –100 
atm.
•Catalytic reduction is stereoselective(which means the hydrogen 
adds in a certain manner across a double bond). It takes place 
through synaddition, where the hydrogens add to the same face 
across the double bond resulting in a cisconfiguration.

Carbocation Rearrangements
•In certain cases, migration of a proton or an alkyl group with its 
bonding pair of electrons, takes place from an adjacent carbon atom 
to a positively charged carbon atom of a carbocation.
•This is known as a rearrangement.
•This occurs so as to form a more stable carbocation.
•Affects which major product is formed in an addition reaction.

•For example, in the addition of HClto 3,3-dimethyl-1-butene, only 
17% of the expected product 2-chloro-3,3-dimethylbutane is formed.
•The major product instead is 2-chloro-2,3-dimethylbutane.
•Formation of rearranged product can be accounted for by the 
following mechanism, the key step of which is a type of 
rearrangement called a 1,2-shift.

Mechanism of a 1,2-shift:
Step 1: Add a proton: Proton transfer from the HCl(electrophile) to the 
alkene (nucleophile) gives a 2°carbocation. 
Step 2: Rearrangement of a bond: Migration of a methyl group with its 
bonding electrons from an adjacent carbon gives a more stable 3°
carbocation intermediate. The major movement is that of the bonding 
electron pair with the methyl group following.

Step 3: Reaction of a nucleophile and electrophile to form a new 
covalent bond:Reaction of the 3°carbocation intermediate (an 
electrophile) with chloride ion (a nucleophile) gives the rearranged 
product.

•The driving force for these rearrangememtsare the formation of 
more stable carbocations.
•The 3°carbocation is more stable than the 2°carbocation.
•Rearrangements also occur in the acid-catalysed hydration of alkenes, 
especially when a carbocation formed in the first step can rearrange 
to a more stable carbocation.
•For example, the acid-catalysed hydration of 3-methyl-1-butene gives 
2-methyl-2-butanol. In this example, the group that migrates is a 
hydrogen with its bonding pair of electrons.

Alkynes
•Alkynes are unsaturated hydrocarbons that have one or more C     C
bonds.
•General formula; C
n
H
2n-2
•The simplest alkyne, ethyne, is also known as acetylene.
•Acetylene is commonly used as a fuel in welding torches.
•They are named by identifying the longest continuous chain in the 
molecule containing a triple bond and adding the ending ‘yne’.
•Same rules as those for naming alkenes.

•The chain is numbered from the end closest to the triple bond, and the 
position of the triple bond is given by its lower numbered carbon atom.
•Substituents are given numbers to indicate their locations.

Physical properties of alkynes:
•They are relatively non-polar, similar to alkanes and alkenes.
•Generally insoluble in water.
•They are quite soluble in organic solvents such as ether, acetone, 
chloroform, methanol.
•Many alkynes have unpleasant odors.
