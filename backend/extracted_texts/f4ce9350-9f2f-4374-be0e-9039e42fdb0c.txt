WHAT IS AMYLOSE?
Amylose is a linear polysaccharide 
structure that is formed when D-glucose 
molecules are joined together.
It occurs when one carbon atom of one 
glucose combines with four carbon atom 
of another glucose molecule by a 
glycosidic bond.
These molecules are tightly packed 
together and they do not form any 
branches. 

Amylose
It is the crystallizable form of starch, 
consisting of long unbranched 
polysaccharide chains
It is one of the two components of starch, 
making up approximately 20-30%. 
Amylose is more resistant to digestion 
than other starch molecules and is 
therefore an important form of resistant 
starch

Amylose
Amylose is known as the “starchy, non-
sticky starch” in cooking. 
It does not dissolve in water, and high 
levels of amylose help grains, like rice, 
maintain their shape.
◦It is relatively soluble in water 
In food manufacturing, some companies 
use amylose as a stabilizer and thickener.

Amylose
Consists of a chain of a few thousand to

Amylose is broken down by salivary 
amylase
In the laboratory it can be broken down 
by acid hydrolysis method

RETROGRADATION
Retrogradation is a reaction that takes 
place when the amylose and amylopectin 
chains in cooked, gelatinized starch realign 
themselves as the cooked starch cools. 
◦It is a process in which amylose and 
amylopectin chains disaggregate in gelatinized 
starch paste re-associate to form more 
ordered structures

RETROGRADATION
This is collective processes of dissolved 
starch becoming less soluble are. 
Retrogradation of cooked starch involves 
amylose and amylopectin (Polymer 
constituents)
Amylose undergoing retrogradation at a 
much more rapid rate than does 
amylopectin. 
◦Retrogradation is an ongoing process which 
involves rapid recrystallization of amylose 
followed by a slow one of amylopectin

The rate of retrogradation depends on 
several variables which include:
◦The molecular ratio of amylose to 
amylopectin; (structures of the amylose and 
amylopectin molecules)
◦Storage temperature; 
◦Starch concentration; 
◦Presence and concentration of other 
ingredients, such as surfactants and salts. 
◦Compositions such as water content, lipids 
and anti-staling enzymes

Staling is a chemical and physical process 
in bread and other foods that reduces 
their palatability
It becomes and Leathery

of cookies, cakes and bread that does not 
include microbial contamination. 
◦It is a change in both chemical and physical 
attributes that give bread products a dry, 
leathery texture. 
It is a major problem in the baking 
industry.

What causes staling?
It is caused mainly by 
◦starch retrogradation

➢Starch undergoes swelling or gelatinization when 
heated in the presence of water

◦Protein – starch interaction
◦Gluten transformation (Gluten cross-links in 
the structure of the baked product)

➢Loss of aroma
➢Loss of moisture and flavor
➢Loss in product freshness
➢Changes in mouth feel
➢Development of crumbliness
➢Toughening of the crust

Structure  and Properties
Dextran is an α-1,6-glucan with several glucose side chains, 
which are bound to the main chain of the macromolecule 
primarily through 1,3-linkages but, in part, also by 1,4- 
and1,2-linkages

STRUCTURE AND PROPERTIES
On an average, 95% of the glucose residues are present in the 
main chain. 
Dextran is very soluble in water.
They are also soluble in other organic solvent
Their viscosity increases with molecular weight
They are biodegradable
They are neutral
They are stable for more than 5 yrs
They are easily filtered

Uses of dextran
Dextran is used mostly in medicine as a blood substitute.  
(antiplatelet to reduce blood viscosity)
In the food industry it is used as a thickening and stabilizing 
agent, as exemplified by its use in baking products, 
confections, beverages and in the production of ice creams.
Used in some eye drop as a lubricant
Used in immobilization in biosensors

Uses of carrageenan
Carrageenan utilization in food processing is based on the ability 
of the polymer 
to gel,
to increase solution viscosity and 
to stabilize
emulsions and 
various dispersions. 
Carrageenans prevent syneresis in fresh cheese
It improve dough properties 
Enable a higher amount of milk powder incorporation in baking. 

Uses of carrageenan
The gelling property in the presence of K+ salt is utilized in 
desserts and canned meat. 
Protein fiber texture is also improved.
Protein sedimentation in condensed milk is prevented by 
carrageenans which, like κ-casein,
prevent milk protein coagulation by calcium ions.
Carrageenans are also used to stabilize ice cream and clarify 
beverages.
