Definition of selection
•Selection is the differential contribution of 
genetic material from one generation to the next. 
•You allow some individuals to contribute their 
genes to the production of the next progeny
•Selection does not create new genes.
•It just allows possessors of some genes to 
produce more offspring than those that do not 
possess the genes 
–so that the frequency of the genes is increased.

Selection for autosomal genes
•If the desired trait is produced by a 
homozygous genotype, 
–it is possible to produce a true breeding 
population by culling (selecting against) 
the undesirable phenotypes.
–This is difficult for a homozygous 
dominant genotype. 
•If the desired phenotype is coded for 
by a heterozygous genotype, 
–it is impossible to produce a true 
breeding population by selection. 
20

Selection for epistatic autosomal 
genes 
•If the desired trait is produced by more than one 
genotype and one or more of the genotypes are 
heterozygous, 
–it is also impossible to produce a true breeding 
population by selection. 
•Selection cannot fix a heterozygous phenotype 
because heterozygotes do not breed true. 
•The only way to produce 100% heterozygotes is 
to breed the recessive genotype with the 
dominant (homozygous) genotype
21

Selection for alleles with 
complete dominance 
•In this case, selection can 
easily fix the recessive 
phenotype and produce a 
true-breeding population, 
but it cannot fix the 
dominant phenotype easily.
22

generation. 
•You just cull the animals with

Selection for the dominant

Progeny testing
•Progeny testing  is the determination of the 
genotype of an individual, or its breeding value, 
based on the phenotypes of its offspring.
•It may be used to identify and cull heterozygotes, 
thereby increasing the frequency of the dominant 
allele. 
•In this technique, we use a test animal (an 
animal whose genotype is known) 
•The  test animal is mated to another animal 
(whose genotype is not (completely) known) 
•The phenotypic ratios of the offspring are 
determined. 
31

Progeny testing
•The technique distinguishes homozygous 
dominant individual from heterozygous ones 
•Because phenotypic ratios are determined by 
probabilities, 
–We must find the number of offspring that  
must have the dominant phenotype, without 
the recessive phenotype,
–in order to reject the null hypothesis (that the 
animal is not homozygous) and declare that 
the animal is truly homozygous, 
–depending on the selected confidence level. 
•The more valuable the animal, the higher the 
confidence interval.
