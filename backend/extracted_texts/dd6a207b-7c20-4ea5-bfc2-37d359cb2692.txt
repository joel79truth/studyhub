Introduction
•All animals show a wide range of 
differences from each other. 
•Even for identical twins, the 
environment in which they are 
raised can never be the same, 
strictly.

Causes of variation
•Large number of genes
•Random segregation of genes
•Independent assortment of genes
•Crossing over
•Environment

Importance of variation
•Variation between individuals is the basis 
for improvement: 
–if all animals were identical genetically
–there would be no way for 
improvement of some traits in 
preference for others except by 
improving the environment.

Variation in qualitative traits
•qualitative traits do not vary 
extensively because 
–they are affected by few genes and
–their expression is relatively discrete, 
•For example, 
–an individual is normally either male or 
female, and
–the range of coat colour is quite small. 

Variation of quantitative traits
•Most traits of economic 
importance are quantitative. 
•A wide and continuous range of 
phenotypes is possible due to 
–The acting together of many genes, 
in association with environmental 
factors

Variance components of an individual’s 
phenotype
•Variation in animal performance is in 
the broad sense due to the effect of 
–heredity and 
–the environment.
•Phenotype can therefore be viewed 
as a function of genotype and

Additive genetic variation, V
A
•The variation due to the additive 
action of alleles is transmitted to the 
next generation in a predictable 
manner. 
•It is never disrupted during meiosis. 
•Additive genetic variation is 
exploited in animal breeding 
programmes.

Dominant genetic variation, V
D
•Variation due to the dominance 
action at a locus.
•It is destroyed during meiosis. 
•It cannot be inherited; it is 
created anew in the new 
generation.

Epistatic genetic variation, V
I
•The variation due to the 
interaction of alleles at two or 
more loci.
•It can also not be inherited; 
–it is destroyed during meiosis and 
created anew in the new 
generation.

Summary of the genotypic variance 
components
•V
D
and V
I
are difficult to quantify 
•They are therefore assumed to be 
equal to zero in large populations 
or in large sample sizes.

Environmental variance, V
E
•This is composed of all the external and 
internal influences on the animal that affect 
its performance. 
•Examples are 
–feed, 
–management practices, 
–the occurrence and control of diseases, 
–Climate
–Weather, etc

Environmental variance, V
E
•The environmental effects can be 
measured at any point in time 
(How?). 
•Some of the effects may be more 
or less permanent during the 
lifetime of an individual, while 
other effects are short-lived.

–the standard deviations for other populations, or
–other traits or 
–standard deviations for the same trait but in 
different age groups. 
•It is a measure of variation in relative terms, and

Analysis of variance
•This is a statistical procedure 
which is used to test the 
significance of the ratio between
–the variance attributable to a given 
factor to 
–the variance due to random error. 
•The test statistic is F.

Covariance
•Covariance is a measure of how much two 
random variables vary together. 
•It’s similar to variance, but where variance tells 
you how a single variable varies, co variance tells 
you how two variables vary together.

Covariance
•A large covariance can mean a strong relationship 
between variables. 
•However, you can’t compare variances over data 
sets with different scales (like pounds and inches). 
•A weak covariance in one data set may be a strong 
one in a different data set with different scales.

•Regression: technique

some variables by knowing 
others
•The process of predicting 
variable Y using variable X

Regression analysis
•Uses a variable (x) to predict 
some outcome variable (y)
•Tells you how values in y 
change as a function of 
changes in values of x

Regression analysis 
➢Calculates the “best-fit” line for a 
certain set of data
•The regression line makes the 
sum of the squares of the 
residuals smaller than for any 
other line
•Regression minimizes residuals
