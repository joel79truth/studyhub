UNITS OF MEASURE
A unit of measure is a well known  value of a 
quantity used to compare with unknown values 
of the quantity.
A quantity’s value known with sufficient 
accuracy in a unit of measure is called the 
standardfor that unit of measure.
Example:
A meter is the unit length and is equal to 
1/299 797 458 of the distance traveled by 
light in vacuum in one second.

analysis

MEASUREMENT AND 
ACURACY
Measuring, Accuracy, Precision, Error

Measuring
To measure is to compare a 
quantity with an unknown value 
to a quantity with a known value 
that is of similar kind to the 
quantity with the unknown 
value.
A measuring device must have a 
property that is affected by the 
quantity that it measures

Accuracy Error and Precision 
Accuracy is the degree of closeness on a 
measured value to the true value of the 
measured quantity.
Erroris the departure of the measured 
value from the true value of the 
measured quantity.
Precisionis the closeness of values of 
repeated measurements. 
Precision is independent of accuracy

Accuracy and Precision 
Precision indicates how 
reproducible the same 
measurement is under equivalent 
circumstances. 
Accuracy of a measurement 
indicates how close the observed 
value is to the true value of the 
measured quantity.



Sources of Error
Observer
Measuring instrument
Environment
Representative sample

Types of Error
Systematic Error:
Involves inaccuracies that tend to occur 
more on one direction than on the 
opposite direction.  
The inaccuracies form a consistent 
pattern
The error is difficult to identify
The error can not be reduced by taking 
an average of repeated measurements

Forms of Systematic Error:
Anoffset Systematic erroroccurs when 
measurementsconsistently differ from the 
true values by a constant value
It’s also called an additive error or a zero-
setting error.



Ascale factor Systematic 
errorinvolevs measurements 
consistently differingfrom the 
true value proportionally
It’s also referred to as a 
correlational systematic error 
or a multiplier error.
The proportionality factor may 
be linear or exponential



Reducing Systematic Error
Systematicerrors can be reducedby:
Triangulation
Using multiple techniques to record 
observations so that you are not relying 
on only one instrument or method.

Regular calibration
Calibrating an instrument means comparing 
what the instrument records with the most 
accuratevalue of theknown, standard of the 
quantity. 
You can also calibrate observers in terms of 
how they code or record data
by usingstandard protocols and routine 
checks to avoid observer carelessness.

Randomization
Randomsampling methodshelp ensure 
that your sample doesn’t systematically 
differ from the population.

Random Error:
Involves inaccuracies that occur in both 
the positive direction and the negative 
direction with equal probability. 
The error is easy to identify 
The error can be reduced by taking an 
average of repeated measurements.



Reducing RandomError
Random error is almost always present in 
measurement, even in highly controlled 
settings. 
Random error canbereducedusing the 
following methods.
Take repeated measurements
A simple way to increase precision is by 
taking repeated measurements and using 
their average

Increase sample size
Large samples have less random error than 
small samples. 
That’s because the errors in different 
directions cancel each other out more 
efficiently when you have more data 
points. 
Collecting data from a large sample 
increases precision.

Control variables
Carefully controlling anyextraneous 
variablesthat could impact 
measurements. 
This removesmajor sources of random 
error.

Random Error and SystematicError
Random error mainlyaffectsprecision, 
How reproducible the same 
measurement is under equivalent 
circumstances. 
In contrast, systematic error affects 
theaccuracyof a measurement, 
How close the observed value is to the 
true value of the measured quantity.

The Limit of Accuracy (Limiting Error)
Measured values always have an uncertainty, 
The values on the scale of a measuring 
instrument can not exactly match the true 
values of the measured quantities.
When recording or using measured values, 
it is important to consider the  reasonable 
range of values within which the true value 
of the measured quantity lies. 

For example, if a kitchen unit is 600 mm 
wide to the nearest 10 mm, it could actually 
be any width from 595 mm up to 605 mm –so 
it might not fit into a gap that is exactly 600 
mm wide. 
To describe range of values within which the 
true value of the measured quantity lies, we 
use it’s lower and upper bounds.

The Lower Bound
Thelower boundis the smallest value 
that canround up to the valueon the 
scale nearest to the measured value.
Thelower boundis the smallest value 
that canround up to the nearest value-of 
the-smallest-divisionon the scale.
The smallest-division is also called the 
degree of accuracyorresolution of 
measuring the instrument

Theupper bound
Theupper boundis the smallestvalue that 
can beround upto thenext valueon the 
scale nearest to the measured value.
Theupper boundis the smallest value that 
can beround up to the value next  to and 
greater than the value-of the-smallest-
divisionnearestto the measured valueon 
the scale.

Example:
The upper bound and the lower bound for 
a mass recorded as 70 kg on a scale with 
a degree of accuracy of 10kg:
a mass of 70 kg, rounded to the nearest 
10 kg, 
has a lower bound of 65 kg, 
because 65 kg is the smallest mass that 
rounds to 70 kg. 

The upper bound is 75 kg, because 75 
kg is the smallest mass that would 
round up to 80kg.
This can be shown as an error interval 
(usinginequality symbols):
65kg≤weight<75kg.


To calculate upper and lower bounds

For the upper limit,halve the degree of 
accuracy, then add this to the value on the 
scale nearestto the measured valueand 

For the lower limit,subtract the half of the 
degree of accuracyfrom the value on the 
scale nearestto the measured value.

The half of the degree of accuracy is the
limiting error (the limit of accuracy)

The limiting error is presented wit the 
±
sign
For example 0.7

Stress-Strain Curve
We can learn about the elastic properties of 
materials by studying the stress-strain 
relationships, under different stress loads, in 
these materials.
The material’s stress-strain curve gives its 
stress-strain relationship. 
In a stress-strain curve, the stress is plotted 
against its corresponding strain values. 
An example of a stress-strain curve is given 
as follows.



The different regions in the stress-strain diagram are:
(A) Proportional Limit
It is the region in the stress-strain curve that obeys 
Hooke’s Law. In this limit, the stress-strain ratio 
gives us a proportionality constant known as 
Young’s modulus. The point OA in the graph 
represents the proportional limit.
(B) Elastic Limit
It is the point in the graph up to which the 
material returns to its original position when the 
load acting on it is completely removed. Beyond 
this limit, the material doesn’t return to its 
original position, and a plastic deformation starts 
to appear in it.

(C) Yield Point
The yield point is defined as the point at which the 
material starts to deform plastically. After the yield 
point is passed, permanent plastic deformation occurs. 
There are two yield points 
(i) upper yield point 
(ii) lower yield point.
(D) Ultimate Stress Point
It is a point that represents the maximum stress that a 
material can endure before failure. Beyond this point, 
failure occurs. Also called ULTIMATE STRENGTH
(E) Fracture or Breaking Point
It is the point in the stress-strain curve at which the 
failure of the material takes place.

Surface Tension
Cohesive forces between molecules cause the 
surface of a liquid to contract to the smallest 
possible surface area. 
This general effect is causes surface tension. 

Molecules on the surface are pulled inward by 
the cohesive forces, reducing the surface 
area.
Molecules inside the liquid experience zero 
net force, since they have neighbors attracting 
them from all sides.

The surface molecules pull each other together 
by the cohesive forces while being pressed 
together due to the cohesive forces between 
them and the molecules inside the liquid
There fore it is hard for an object to break 
through a liquid surface

The liquid surface acts like a stretched elastic 
sheet. 
The cohesive forces along the liquid surface 
forma a tension force along the surface

stretched surface
