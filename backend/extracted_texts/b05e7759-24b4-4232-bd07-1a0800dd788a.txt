

Air Pollution Concepts
1. Composition of Atmosphere
The Earth's atmosphere is a layer of gases surrounding the planet, held in place by gravity. It is composed of:
- Nitrogen (N2) - 78%: The most abundant gas, essential for plant life (through nitrogen fixation).
- Oxygen (O2) - 21%: Vital for respiration in most living organisms.
- Argon (Ar) - 0.93%: An inert noble gas with minimal biological activity.
- Carbon Dioxide (CO2) - 0.04%: Important for photosynthesis; a greenhouse gas.
- Trace gases: Including neon, helium, methane, krypton, and hydrogen.
- Water vapor: Varies by location and weather (0-4%).
These   components   are   normally   in   balance,   but   pollutants   can   alter   this   composition,   leading   to
environmental and health issues.
2. Aerosols
Aerosols  are  tiny  solid  or  liquid  particles  suspended  in  the  atmosphere.  They  come  from  both  natural  and
human-made sources and can affect climate, air quality, and health.
Types of aerosols:
- Natural: Sea salt, volcanic ash, dust, pollen, forest fire particles.
- Anthropogenic (man-made): Industrial emissions, vehicle exhaust, combustion of fossil fuels.
Effects:
- Reflect or absorb sunlight (cooling or warming effect on climate).
- Serve as nuclei for cloud formation.
- Penetrate the lungs and cause respiratory issues.
3. Sources of Pollution
Air pollution can be categorized by source:
a. Natural Sources:

- Volcanic eruptions (sulfur dioxide, ash).
- Forest fires (carbon monoxide, particulate matter).
- Dust storms.
- Pollen and spores.
b. Anthropogenic (Human-caused) Sources:
- Industrial emissions: Sulfur dioxide, nitrogen oxides, volatile organic compounds (VOCs).
- Transportation: Carbon monoxide, nitrogen oxides, hydrocarbons.
- Agriculture: Methane (from livestock), ammonia (from fertilizers).
- Domestic sources: Burning wood, coal, or waste for cooking/heating.
4. Improving Air Quality
Air  quality  improvement  involves  reducing  the  release  of  harmful  pollutants  into  the  atmosphere.  Key
strategies include:
- Regulating emissions from factories and power plants.
- Transitioning to cleaner energy sources (solar, wind, hydroelectric).
- Improving fuel standards and vehicle emission norms.
- Promoting public transportation and electric vehicles.
- Urban planning with more green spaces to absorb pollutants.
- Monitoring and enforcement using air quality sensors and legal penalties.
5. Air Quality Index (AQI)
The Air Quality Index (AQI) is a standardized tool to measure and communicate how polluted the air currently
is  or  how  polluted  it  is  forecast  to  become.  It  simplifies  complex  air  pollution  data  into  a  single  number  and
color code.
Key pollutants measured:

- Particulate Matter (PM2.5 and PM10)
- Ozone (O3)
- Nitrogen Dioxide (NO2)
- Sulfur Dioxide (SO2)
- Carbon Monoxide (CO)
AQI scale (example):
- 0-50: Good
- 51-100: Moderate
- 101-150: Unhealthy for sensitive groups
- 151-200: Unhealthy
- 201-300: Very unhealthy
- 301-500: Hazardous
6. Reducing Air Pollution
Air pollution can be reduced through individual and collective actions:
Government Actions:
- Enforcing environmental laws and emission limits.
- Investing in renewable energy.
- Expanding public transport.
Community Actions:
- Tree planting campaigns.
- Waste management and recycling programs.
Individual Actions:
- Using energy-efficient appliances.
- Carpooling, cycling, or using public transport.
