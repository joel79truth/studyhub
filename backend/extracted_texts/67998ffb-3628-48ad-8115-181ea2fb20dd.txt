

1. Cell Division
Mitosis
A type of nuclear division where one cell divides to form two identical daughter cells, maintaining the 
chromosome number (2n ŌåÆ 2n).
Stages
1. Prophase: Chromosomes condense; spindle fibers begin to form; nuclear envelope starts breaking 
down.
2. Metaphase: Chromosomes align at the metaphase plate; spindle fibers attach to centromeres.
3. Anaphase: Sister chromatids are pulled apart to opposite poles.
4. Telophase: Chromosomes de-condense; nuclear envelope reforms.
5. Cytokinesis: Cytoplasm divides, producing two identical cells.
Importance in plants and animals
’āśGrowth of tissues/organs.
’āśRepair of damaged cells.
’āśAsexual reproduction in plants (e.g., potato tubers, banana suckers).
Meiosis
Reduction division where a diploid (2n) cell produces four haploid (n) gametes.
Meiosis I (Reduction Division): homologous chromosomes separate. Crossing-over occurs in prophase I 
ŌåÆ introduces genetic variation.
Meiosis II (Equational Division): similar to mitosis, separates sister chromatids.
Importance
’āśMaintains chromosome number across generations.
’āśCreates variation through recombination and independent assortment.
’āśBasis for plant and animal breeding.
