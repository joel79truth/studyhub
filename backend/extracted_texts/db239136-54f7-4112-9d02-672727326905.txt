Geometric isomers of alkenes
•Rotation about C=C double bond is severely restricted, due to the 
presence of the pi bond.
•This is unlike rotation about C—C single bond; rotation is relatively 
easy and these bonds are free to rotate. 
•Due to the restriction of rotation about the double bond, alkenes 
which have two different groups bonded to each carbon atom show 
cis-trans isomerism.

•The cis-trans isomers are geometric isomers - same molecular formula 
and same groups bonded to one another but differ in the spatial 
arrangement of the groups.
• In a cis isomer, the two groups are on the same side of the double 
bond. In a trans isomer, the two groups are on opposite sides of the 
double bond.

The E,Z system
•The E,Z system must be used for tri, and tetra substituted alkenes. It 
is used to specify the configuration of groups about a C=C double 
bond.
•The system uses a set of rules to assign priorities to substituents on 
each carbon of the double bond.
•If the groups of higher priority are on the same side of the double 
bond, the configuration assigned is Z ( Z: from German word 
zusammen meaning same side).
•If the groups of higher priority are on opposite sides of the double 
bond, the configuration assigned is E ( E: from German word 
entgegen meaning opposite).

The first step in assigning an E or Z configuration is to 
label each substituent on each carbon of the double 
bond in terms of priorities.

Cycloalkenes
•To name cycloalkenes, we number the carbon atoms of the ring 
double bond 1 and 2 in the direction that gives the substituent 
encountered first the lowest number.
•We name and locate substituents, and list them in alphabetical order.

Physical properties of alkenes
•Alkenes, just like alkanes and alkynes, are non-polar.
•They only have dispersion forces between their molecules, therefore 
similar physical properties as alkanes.
•They are insoluble in water, and insoluble in polar organic solvents lie 
ethanol.

Commercial importance of alkenes
•Due to their reactivity, alkenes are useful intermediates in the 
synthesis of plastics, dyes, pesticides.
•Ethene (ethylene) is used to make polyethylene, ethanol, acetic acid 
and a variety of other organic chemicals.
•It is also the plant hormone that speeds up the ripening of fruits.
•Propene is used in the manufacture of polypropylene, acetone, and 
isopropyl alcohol.
•Most common polymers are made by polymerising alkenes.

Stability of alkenes
•Trans alkenes are more stable than cis alkenes (less steric hindrance 
of substituents).
•The more substituted an alkene double bond, the more stable the 
alkene will be.
•Alkyl groups attached to double bonded carbons stabilise the alkene.

•Double bonds that are separated from each other by just one single bond 
are said to be conjugated.
•Double bonds that are separated from each other by more than one single 
bond are said to be isolated.
•The stability of conjugated alkenes compared to isolated alkenes is due to
