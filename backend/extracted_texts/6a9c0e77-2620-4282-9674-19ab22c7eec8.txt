EXPERIMENT # 2: OHM’S LAW AND RESISTANCE 
AIM 
To determine the resistance per cm of a given wire by plotting a graph of potential difference 
versus current and determining its resistivity. 
 
THEORY 
The material can resist the flow of the charges, and the measure of how much a material 
resists the flow of charges is known as resistivity. The resistance is a measure of how difficult 
it is to pass current through a wire or component. Resistance depends on the resistivity. The 
resistivity is a characteristic of the material used to fabricate a wire or other electrical 
component, whereas the resistance is a characteristic of the wire or component. 
There are three types of materials; conductors, semiconductors, and insulators.

PROCEDURES 
1. First draw the circuit diagram. 
2. Arrange the apparatus in the same manner as given in the arrangement diagram. 
3. Clean the ends of the connecting wires with sandpaper to remove insulation, if any. 
4. Make neat, clean, and tight connections according to the circuit diagrams. While 
making connections ensure that +ve marked terminals of the voltmeter and ammeter 
are joined towards the +ve terminals of the battery. 
5. Determine the least count of the voltmeter and ammeter, and also note the zero 
error, if any. 
6. Insert the key K, slide the rheostat contact, and see that the ammeter and voltmeter 
are working properly. 
7. Adjust the sliding contact of the rheostat such that a small current passes through the 
resistance coil or the resistance wire. 
8. Note down the value of the potential difference V from the voltmeter and current I 
from the ammeter. 
9. Shift the rheostat contact slightly so that both the ammeter and voltmeter show full 
division readings and not in fractions. 
10. Record the readings of the voltmeter and ammeter. 
11. Take at least six sets of independent observations. 
12. Record the observations in a tabular column 
13. Now, cut the resistance wire at the points where it leaves the terminals, stretch it, and 
find its length by the meter scale. 
14. Then find out the diameter and hence the radius of the wire using the screw gauge 
and calculate the cross-sectional area A. 
15. Plot a graph between current (I) along the X-axis and potential difference across the 
wire (V) along Y-axis 
16. Determine the slope of the graph. The slope will give the value of resistance (R) of the 
material of the wire. 
17. Calculate the resistance per centimeters of the wire.
