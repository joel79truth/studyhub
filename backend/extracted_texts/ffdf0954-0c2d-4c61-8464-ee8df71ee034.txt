FUNCTION OF PARTS OF RUMINANT 
1. RUMEN: (pouch) 
The largest of the four stomach compartments of ruminants 
and its functions are: 
’é© To store roughage temporarily before it is returned to the 
mouth for rumination, i.e. for chewing cud. 
’é© The food remains there being broken up, softened and 
fermented. 
’é© The rumen contains bacteria, fungi and protozoa that 
break down cellulose into products of nutritional value 
e.g. organic acids. These organisms use non-protein 
nitrogen compounds such as urea to make amino acids. 
’é© Members of the vitamin B6 complex such as vitamin B 
(Thiamine). Vitamin B2 (Lactoflavin) and vitamin B6 
(Pyridoxine) are synthesized in the rumen. 
 
2. RETICULAM (honeycomb) 
This is the second compartment of a ruminant stomach 
where bacterial action continues. It has a honeycomb 

texture thus the name honeycomb. This compartment sieves 
or separates finely ground material from coarse ones. 
Secondly, foreign material such as stones or hand pieces of 
wood are retained therein before the food is passed on to 
the Omasum. 
 
3. OMASUM (manyplies) 
This is the third compartment of a ruminant stomach. It 
contains a mass of suspended parallel rough ŌĆō  surface 
ŌĆś  leavesŌĆÖ   which grind food to a fine consistency. The food 
particles are temporarily stored here before being ground. 
Water is absorbed from food on its way to the abomasum. 
 
4. ABOMASUM 
This is the fourth compartment where enzymatic digestion 
takes place. It is this region that digestion of proteins starts. 
Pepsin converts protein into proteoses, peptones and 
polypeptides. As in young monogastric animals, rennin acts 
upon milk protein, casein, to curdle milk, a form, which 
enables it to stay a long time in the abomasum until it, is 
broken down to amino acids. Some harmful bacteria are 
killed by the hydrochloric acid found in this compartment. 
From here onwards digestion is the same as in non ŌĆō 
ruminants

Milk let down; 
Milk removal includes; 
’Ć░Milk ejection from milk alveolar lumens into ducts 
following a neurohomonal reflex 
’Ć░The withdrawal of milk from the cisterns and ducts 
of the mammary gland. 
The milk ejection is a result of contraction of the cells 
(myoepitherial) under the influence of the hormone called 
oxytocin . This accompanies active withdrawal by 
sucking/milking. Or passive withdrawal (leaking). Leaking 
is because of high intermammary pressure.  
 
Milk let down is inhibited by release of adrenaline 
produced as a result of fright or emotional disturbance. 
Adrenaline not only prevents the release of oxytocin but 
also causes vasoconstriction of blood vessels leading to the 
mammary gland, this preventing oxytocin reaching the 
myoepitherial cells.

Calf rearing methods 
1. Single suckling method 
The calf stays with the dam for 6 months or more so that 
the calf should suckle freely and this is done in beef 

production and as a result it produces high calf growth 
rates. 
 
2.  Restricted suckling 
Dairy calves are allowed to suckle at only certain times of 
the day. The cow and the calf are separated overnight and 
the cow is milked in the morning. The calf suckles for 
about 1- 2 minutes to stimulate milk let down and is then 
suckled after milking. 
 
Advantages 
’Ć░it stimulates milk let down 
’Ć░improves calf growth 
’Ć░may reduce mastitis 
’Ć░Cow will show all round performance in terms of calf 
growth and milk yield.

feeding) or from an artificial teat dispenser. This particular 
method requires good management otherwise calves are

Calves be fed three times a day for the first month. Too

Preparaton for parturition (calving) 
The cow becomes uneasy and moves away from the rest of

’Ć░distension of the teats and udder

TYPES OF PASTURE 
- Natural pasture (grasses or Legumes) 
- Sown pastures (grasses or Legumes) 
 
Establishement of sown pastures 
Depends on variety and growth habit. It can be done using; 
seeds, roots, splits (rhizomes) and setts (stem cuttings) 
 
METHODS OF ESTABLISHMENT 
 

1) SEED ESTABLISHMENT 
Undersowing ŌĆō  growing Rhodes grass under maize plant 
and its planted soon after the maize is 30cm high 
 
Adv 
- Considerable economic advantage where an extra crop 
of maize is grown 
- The cost of preparing a seed bed specially for the 
pasture is avoided. 
- Reduces weed competition between maize plant and 
some species 
- The maize protects the young pasture plants against 
adverse conditions such as high temperature and strong 
winds 
- In mixed swards, the shade from the maize alters the 
competitive balance favour of the legume against the 
grass in the establishing sward.
