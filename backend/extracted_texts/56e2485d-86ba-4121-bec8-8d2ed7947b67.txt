•Nutrition
1.It is the science of food, the nutrients and other 
substances there in, their interaction to health 
and disease and the process by which organisms 
ingest, digest, absorb, transport, utilize and 
excrete food substances.
2.The process by which the living organism receives 
and utilizes food.
Nutrition is based on fundamental principles of 
chemistry, biology, microbiology, anatomy and 
physiology and biochemistry.

•Food
•Substance eaten to fulfill needs for maintenance, 
growth, work and repair of tissues. 
•Staple Food
•A food eaten regularly and in enough quantities to 
furnish a substantial proportion of the total energy 
supply. 
•Diet
•The usual food and drink consumed by an individual 
or a population group.

•Balanced diet
•It is a diet which provides or meets all the 
nutritional requirements of the body.
•Nutrients
•These are compounds found in foods which are used 
in normal metabolism of the body. 
•Nutritional Status
1.The condition resulting from the balance between 
the supply of nutrients on the one hand and the 
expenditure of nutrients on the other hand.
2.Condition of the body resulting from food intake, 
absorption and metabolism.

Principle 4: Nutrient Density
Assessment of the nutritional quality 
of an individual food
Compare vitamin/mineral content with 
amount of energy provided
Nutrient dense food: provides large 
amount of a nutrient for a relatively 
small amount of energy
The higher a food’s nutrient density, 
the better it is as a nutrient source

An Example of a Recipe
Procedure 
•
Select ripe and firm fruits.
•
Wash the fruits with clean running water.
•
Cut the plums into quarts and add sugar and ¼ cup of water and dissolve the mixture in 
a pot.
•
Add lemon juice and place the pot over medium heat and simmer the plums (do not 
cover the pot), while stirring continuously.  
•
Use a masher to break the big chunks of plum into smaller pieces
•
The mixture will start with big, juicy bubbles and slowly progress to small, tighter 
bubbles as the jam gets closer to doneness.
•
Remove the jam from the heat and transfer to the clean jars once it is done. Test for 
doneness-drop some jam into a glass of cold water, if the jam disintegrates then it is 
not done.
•
Cover and cool completely before moving the jam to the fridge for long-term storage.

Steaming
•Steam rather than liquid cooks the food. Food is 
placed on a rack over boiling water.
•Used for delicate foods such as fish and vegetables. 
The steam penetrates the food and not the water 
itself. The food is covered in steam and heat from 
steam hits the food molecules that vibrate due to heat 
energy applied. Thus steam cooks food.
•Most root crops such as cassava, carrots, and potatoes 
maintain their nutrients and colour through this 
method of cooking. 
•Leafy vegetables lose colour if cooked this way. 
•Food can also be steamed in pressure cooker
•Food is put in a pressure pan and heat is applied. 
Water is added before food is put in the pan. Mostly 
used when you want to maintain flavour and colour
